How to Use This System for Annual Rent Increases

A step-by-step guide for the annual rent-setting process

The point of this tool isn't the process — you already know the process. The point is the guardrails. It catches the mistakes before they become compliance problems: a LIHTC property where your proposed rent blows past the AMI ceiling, a property where the increase doesn't cover your insurance jump, a DSCR that's sliding below your lender's covenant. You set the rents; the system checks your work.

Below is how the tabs map to the annual rent-setting workflow, in the order most operators actually do it.

1

Glance at the portfolio

Portfolio tab →

Before touching individual rents, spend 30 seconds on the big picture. Total units, average rent, total NOI, how many properties carry rent restrictions. If the Preservation Pipeline chart shows properties expiring soon, those need a different strategy than the rest.

Healthy overall, or pockets under stress? The answer decides whether you go uniform or property by property.

2

Check the market

Market tab →

The Market tab shows HUD Fair Market Rent trajectories — the closest thing to a government "what should rent be" benchmark. If FMR has climbed 5% per year and you've been raising 2%, there's room. If it's flat, the market is telling you something.

Rule of thumb: 3–5% is standard in a healthy market. Above 5% needs a specific justification — renovations, cost pressure, or deep below-market positioning.

3

Set your increases — bulk first, then adjust

Rent Planner tab →

This is the core workflow. Most operators do it in this order:

  1. Bulk default first. Use the controls at top: "3% on all MHP," then "2% on all LIHTC." Select the type, enter the percentage, click Apply.
  2. Review the KPIs. The four summary cards update instantly — total revenue impact, NOI impact, properties at AMI ceiling, new average rent.
  3. Override individuals. Click into any property's Increase % cell and type a different number. A park that had a rough year gets 0%. One that's deeply below market gets 5%. KPIs recalculate as you edit.
  4. Filter to focus. Use the state/type dropdowns. When you filter to "FL only" or "LIHTC only," the KPIs recalculate for just that subset.
  5. Click any row for the full picture. The detail modal shows expenses, DSCR, cash flow, AMI ceiling, acquisition price — everything you need to justify the number.
4

Check the guardrails

Rent Planner tab →

Before you finalize, scan for three things:

  • Red rows — the proposed rent hit the AMI ceiling. You can't go higher. If you need more NOI from these properties, the lever is expenses, not rent.
  • DSCR below 1.15x — open the detail modal. If projected DSCR is below your lender's covenant, you may need a larger increase or you need to cut expenses.
  • Rent vs. FMR — if you're significantly above FMR, vacancy risk goes up. If you're well below, there's room you're leaving on the table.

This is the step where the tool earns its keep. In a spreadsheet, none of these violations surface automatically — you'd have to check each one by hand.

5

Stress-test individual properties

Rent Decision tab →

For any property you're uncertain about, switch to the Rent Decision tab and select it from the dropdown. This is where you model the full picture:

  • FMR 2BR — the market comp for that county
  • AMI ceiling + headroom — exactly how much room you have
  • Your expense assumptions — enter real numbers for insurance, taxes, CapEx, and turnover for this specific property

The waterfall chart updates live: watch Gross Revenue flow through vacancy, expenses, and debt service to Cash Flow. Drag the increase slider and see the bars move.

This is the "what if" screen. Try 3%. Try 5%. Try 0%. See what each does to the bottom line before you commit.

6

Know your LIHTC ceilings

Rent Decision tab →

For any property flagged LIHTC, there is a maximum allowable rent calculated from HUD's annual income limits: 30% of the applicable AMI tier (50%, 60%, or 80%) divided by 12 months. The system computes this for you.

Important: HUD publishes new income limits every spring (usually April). Your ceiling may go up year over year — check the new limits before finalizing. This system uses FY 2026 limits.

If a property is already at or above its ceiling, the system holds the current rent and flags it. You cannot raise further under LIHTC rules.

7

Export and hand off

Export CSV button — top right of every page

Click Export CSV. The file includes every visible property with: name, state, type, units, current rent, increase %, proposed rent, FMR, AMI ceiling, NOI, debt service, and DSCR. Hand it to:

  • Your accountant — for budget projections and tax planning
  • Your property manager — to prepare tenant notice letters. Remember: most states require 30–90 days written notice before an increase takes effect.
  • Your lender — if covenant compliance is tight, the projected DSCR column shows you're managing it

The CSV opens in Excel. From there your property manager can sort by lease-expiration date and schedule the notices.

8

Timing: leases, notices, and limits

Reference

The tool decides what to charge. The when depends on your leases and your state:

  • Apartments (annual leases): increases take effect at lease renewal. Best practice: 60–90 days written notice before the renewal date. You can't raise rent on a tenant mid-lease unless the lease has an escalator clause.
  • MHP lot rents: varies by state — 30 days notice in some, 90 in others. Some states cap annual increases (Oregon: 10% + CPI). Check your state statute.
  • LIHTC: rents can be raised when HUD publishes new AMI figures (usually April), but most operators wait for lease renewal.

Leases in progress: you can't raise rent on everyone January 1. Sort your export by lease-expiration date to see which tenants renew when. That determines your implementation schedule — some increases take effect in March, others in September. Plan accordingly.

About the data in this system

Market data (FMR, AMI, migration) comes from HUD and the IRS — updated annually. Property data (rents, expenses, occupancy) is only as current as the last time it was loaded. Before finalizing, make sure your property data reflects actual current numbers, not last year's.

Every acronym in the system is clickable — if you see a term you don't recognize, click the dotted underline for a plain-English definition.
